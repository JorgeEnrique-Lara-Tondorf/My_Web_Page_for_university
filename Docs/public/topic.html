Agriculture Robotics

Universidad Francisco de Vitoria - Escuela Politecnica Superior

Group 6: Jorge Enrique Lara Tondorf, Jeronimo Gast, Juan Martín-Gil Fanconi, Alfonso Lozano Hernando, Borja Rincón Lozada [1]

1. Introduction & Motivation

Agricultural robotics involves automated machines designed for farming tasks such as planting, harvesting, and crop monitoring with minimal human intervention [2]. These systems utilize technologies like AI, machine learning, and GPS to improve precision and productivity [2].

The team’s motivation was inspired by the film Interstellar, which portrays an autonomous farm where manpower is minimal and efficiency is maximized [2]. The objective is to analyze how these machines can replace repetitive or "boring" tasks currently performed by humans [2, 3].

2. Market Growth and Necessity

The agricultural robot market grew from USD 6.6 billion in 2019 to USD 12.6 billion in 2023 [4]. It is projected to increase by 18.3% during the 2025-2032 period [5]. This growth is driven by the global population increase and a decreasing labor force; the sector must increase productivity by 60% by 2050 to meet food demands [5].

3. The 8 Main Types of Agricultural Robots

Robot Type Core Functionality
Planting and Seeding Automate seed sowing with precise placement, soil digging, and fertilization [6].
Harvesting and Picking Use vision systems to detect ripeness and pick fruits or grains automatically [6].
Drones Act as the "eyes" of the farm, monitoring crops and applying pesticides over large areas [6].
Crop Sprayers Precise delivery of chemicals to specific locations to prevent food contamination and pollution [6].
Weeding and Mowing Identify and remove weeds using lasers, herbicides, or mechanical tools [6].
Sorting and Grading Categorize products by size, color, or quality using cameras and sensors [6].
Autonomous Tractors Handle time-consuming tillage tasks, saving farmers both time and money [6].
Livestock Management Automate milking, feeding, and egg collection to improve efficiency and animal welfare [6].

Market-Leading Examples

  • Lely Astronaut: A leader in automatic milking systems with over 50,000 installations globally [7].
  • FarmWise Titan FT-35: An autonomous weeding robot that uses AI and a continuous learning system trained on millions of images to distinguish crops from weeds [8].
  • John Deere See & Spray: A selective spraying platform capable of taking 2,100 images per second to apply chemicals only where needed [9].

4. Technical Capabilities & Future Trends

Modern robots navigate fields autonomously using LIDAR sensors and wheel encoders, allowing them to move between crop rows without human help [10].

The future of this technology relies on two pillars:

  • Artificial Intelligence (AI): Moves beyond automation to enable robots to learn from data, detect diseases, and recognize growth patterns [11].
  • Internet of Things (IoT): Connects soil sensors, weather stations, and drones into a single smart ecosystem for real-time decision-making [12].

5. Ethical and Social Perspectives

The "4th agricultural revolution" presents several critical challenges: [13]

  • Power Centralization: High costs may favor large corporations, potentially creating monopolies and displacing independent farmers [13, 14].
  • Data Privacy: Concerns exist that companies might use farm productivity data to gain unfair advantages in stock markets [14].
  • Cybersecurity: Connected machinery is vulnerable to hackers, which could lead to accidents or data theft [15].
  • Environmental Impact: While robots reduce chemical waste, heavy machinery can compact the soil, and their manufacturing requires non-renewable materials like lithium [15, 16].
  • Job Displacement: Automated processes may lead to initial layoffs in rural communities before new technical roles are created [16, 17].

6. Conclusion

Agrorobotics offers a sustainable solution for a growing world, but its success depends on collaboration between humans and robots [17, 18]. While technology can handle repetitive and dangerous tasks, human oversight remains essential for planning, data analysis, and ethical regulation [18-20].
